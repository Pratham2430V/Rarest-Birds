World’s Rarest Birds: A 2026 Status Report

Stresemann’s Bristlefront (Merulaxis stresemanni)

Population: Fewer than 15 individuals
Location: Brazil
Status: Critically Endangered
Often cited as the rarest bird in the world, this secretive songbird survives in a tiny fragment of the Atlantic Forest.

Stresemann's bristlefront (Merulaxis stresemanni) is a critically endangered species of bird in the family Rhinocryptidae,[1][2] commonly known as tapaculos. It is endemic to Brazil, where there are 50 or fewer individuals living exclusively in the Mata do Passarinho Reserve.

Taxonomy and systematics

Stresemann's bristlefront and the slaty bristlefront (Merulaxis ater) are sister species,[4] the latter being significantly more common.[5] Merulaxis stresemanni is currently considered monotypic, with no recognized subspecies.
The species is known from only three specimens, along with a handful of photographs, audio recordings, and documented sightings.[4] The first specimen was acquired in the 1830s; subsequent specimens would not be collected until more than a century later.[4] The species is difficult to identify, as is the case for other members of the tapaculo family.

Description

Stresemann's bristlefront is a medium-sized, long-tailed bird with distinctive forehead bristles. It measures 20 cm (7.9 in) long. The male is all slaty gray with a dark rufous-chestnut rump, uppertail-coverts, and vent. The namesake features are long, pointed bristles on the forehead. The female is cinnamon-brown above, with a duskier tail, and is bright cinnamon below.
